Educational Technology

Supporting Self-Directedness

These two examples of educational technology tools an educator can use to foster or support goals of self-directedness and independent student learning.

Tools

Keeping students accountable and on task has always been a challenge in the classroom. Teachers have used resources such as battery operated kitchen timers, token economy and even posted visual supports of what “on task” behavior should look like. When using technology you have the flexibility of adding aids to your activities as well. You can clearly post due dates on their personal calendars if using programs like canvas. When you submit an assignment the posting on the calendar gets crossed out so that students know it is completed. Timers can be added to quizzes and games so that students are staying on task throughout the activity. Many of the games and quizzes that can be added for the students to complete have built in reinforcers when students complete different phases of the assignment. This acts much like a token economy in that when they complete a certain number of questions something fun or interactive happens. They can even earn online stickers. The options for developing content that encourages students to independently complete tasks and to hold themselves accountable for completing tasks are robust. One specific resource I like to use is Kahn academy. Teachers are able to login and actually assign specific topics to students for them to learn on their own. This allows students to be completely self directed and independent when learning the material.

Epic! Is an additional tool that can be used to assist students with self directed learning. This is an application free for teachers that allows them to set goals for students such as a certain number of books to read. Then it allows students to self explore with over 2000 books to choose from.

Assistive Technology

Fostering Independent Learning

These two assistive technologies meets individual student needs and learning in an educational setting.

Assistive Tech

Students come to the educational system with a variety of needs, interests and strengths. But technology has something for pretty much everyone. For students that require visual assistance there are ways to adjust the font to a bigger size. You can use programming that reads the material to the student which assists with students who have visual challenges or even processing challenges. For students that English is not their first language you can use virtual "word walls" that they can click words they don't understand and it shows them a visual example as well explains the term. You can even do this using the students home language.
